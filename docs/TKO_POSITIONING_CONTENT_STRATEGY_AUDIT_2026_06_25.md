## Executive Answer

**TKO is in the business of building AI-enabled operational intelligence systems for complex enterprise workflows.**

More plainly:

> TKO helps healthcare and enterprise operations leaders turn fragmented data, workflow signals, and institutional knowledge into governed decisions, trusted next actions, and measurable execution.

TKO should not position as a generic AI consultancy, workflow automation shop, SaaS company, or traditional management consultancy. The defensible position is:

> **A senior operator-builder for AI-enabled operational intelligence: diagnosing where complex work stalls, then building the decision layer that tells teams what needs attention and what happens next.**

The best market-entry wedge is healthcare payer operations and transformation, especially administrative burden, prior authorization, utilization management, care management, provider operations, interoperability, and AI adoption programs where workflows are complex, regulated, cross-functional, and under cost pressure.

Refined PA wedge: Prior Authorization Operational Assessment is a Tier-1 TKO opportunity. It should be marketed as operational quality improvement before Gold Card, automation, AI, FHIR, claims, or payer technology products. Gold Card readiness is an output of operational quality, not the product.

The founder's advantage is the combination of:

- enterprise healthcare transformation experience
- product/program ownership
- ability to design operating models
- ability to build working systems
- proof from RachelOS
- proof from the CRE Intelligence Factory model
- AI-enabled solo delivery economics

The market should understand TKO as a premium, senior-led alternative to large firms and offshore delivery teams for problems where ambiguity, judgment, workflow design, and implementation discipline matter more than staffing scale.

## 2. What TKO Should Not Be

| Positioning trap | Why it is weak |
|---|---|
| Generic AI consultancy | Crowded, vague, and commoditized. It invites model/tool conversations instead of business-problem conversations. TKO's strongest proof is workflow and decision design, not prompt engineering. |
| AI agent shop | Too close to hype. Enterprise buyers in healthcare need governance, auditability, exception handling, and human approval, not a promise that agents will replace work. |
| Workflow automation agency | Sounds like Zapier, Make, n8n, or offshore RPA implementation. It pushes TKO into low-price execution work and discards senior transformation credibility. |
| Digital transformation consultant | Too broad and owned by Accenture, Deloitte, Cognizant, Slalom, and large SIs. A solo founder cannot win a category defined by scale. |
| Healthcare strategy consultant | Better than generic AI, but still too deck-oriented. It underplays the ability to build the operating layer. |
| SaaS company | Misaligned with founder preference and economics. It introduces user management, billing, support, onboarding, roadmap obligations, and long time-to-revenue. |
| Report platform | CRE reports are output surfaces, not the core business. Positioning around reports fragments TKO and undervalues the intelligence model. |
| "Army of one" as the headline | Useful internally and in sales conversations, but too founder-centric as market positioning. The buyer cares about faster senior execution, fewer handoffs, lower overhead, and better outcomes. |

Use AI as leverage and proof. Do not make it the category.

## 6. Rate and Margin Strategy

Given the stated market/rate context:

- Industry benchmark: **$185/hour**
- UHC internal rate moving from **$145 to $155/hour**
- Offshore rate moving from **$45 to $53/hour**

Implications:

| Rate | Change / meaning |
|---|---|
| $155 internal | About 84% of the $185 benchmark. The internal rate increase narrows the discount between in-house resource cost and premium external specialist cost. |
| $53 offshore | About 29% of the $185 benchmark, but the rate increase raises the cost of low-leverage execution while not solving ambiguity, governance, workflow design, or adoption. |
| $185 benchmark | Useful as an anchor, but TKO should avoid selling hourly whenever possible. |

TKO should use rates as sales logic, not pricing format:

- A $20K diagnostic equals roughly 108 hours at $185/hour, but should be sold as a fixed executive outcome.
- A $75K build equals roughly 405 benchmark hours. If AI-enabled delivery and senior judgment compress delivery into fewer founder hours, TKO captures the leverage instead of passing it away.
- Compared with offshore, TKO should argue that the expensive part of these problems is not coding hours; it is misunderstood work, rework, escalation delay, adoption failure, and ungoverned AI.

Best commercial message:

> Enterprise teams are paying more for internal and offshore capacity, but capacity alone does not create operational intelligence. TKO uses senior healthcare transformation judgment plus AI-enabled delivery to diagnose faster, build with fewer handoffs, and leave behind a governed decision system.

Do not lead with "cheap." Lead with:

- faster time to operational truth
- fewer handoffs
- senior judgment applied directly
- less rework
- human-controlled AI
- fixed-scope outcomes
- lower overhead than large firms

Entry offer.

- Director/VP payer operations
- Director/VP transformation
- VP utilization management
- VP provider operations
- health-tech COO
- consulting partner with payer client

Business problem:

The organization has workflows, systems, dashboards, and meetings, but leadership cannot see where work actually stalls, what decision is missing, or where AI can safely reduce burden.

Deliverables:

- workflow and operating model review
- current-state map
- stakeholder interviews
- decision-rights and escalation assessment
- administrative burden map
- operational visibility gap analysis
- AI/workflow opportunity assessment
- 90-day recovery roadmap
- executive readout

Timeline:

- 2-3 weeks

Pricing:

- $18K-$30K
- First-client wedge can be $10K-$15K if it creates proof or relationship value.

Expected outcome:

- sponsor has a clear, evidence-based view of where work stalls, what matters first, and what should be fixed or built.

### Offer 2: Decision Layer Build Sprint

Core engagement.

Target buyer:

- diagnostic client ready to act
- health-tech team with a known workflow/adoption problem
- payer transformation sponsor with one workflow area to fix

Business problem:

The organization does not need more reporting. It needs a working operational layer that captures facts, shows current state, identifies what needs attention, and routes action through human approval.

Deliverables:

- target workflow design
- operational memory/fact model
- state and prioritization logic
- action queue model
- exception/escalation model
- human-in-the-loop AI design
- prototype or working implementation plan
- adoption cadence
- measurement model
- handoff documentation

Timeline:

- 6-10 weeks for one workflow
- 8-12 weeks for a broader build

Pricing:

- $45K-$90K focused sprint
- $90K-$150K for larger healthcare transformation recovery build

Expected outcome:

- a working or implementation-ready decision layer for one critical workflow, with governance and human approval designed in.

### Offer 3: Fractional Operational Intelligence Advisor

Ongoing advisory engagement.

Target buyer:

- post-diagnostic sponsor
- post-build leadership team
- consulting partner needing senior oversight
- health-tech executive scaling delivery/adoption

Business problem:

The operating system will drift unless someone maintains decision discipline, workflow governance, prioritization, and AI adoption controls.

Deliverables:

- weekly operating review
- priority and risk review
- workflow governance
- AI adoption guidance
- executive sponsor brief
- roadmap adjustment
- vendor/team coordination support
- decision-log and action-model oversight

Timeline:

- 3-6 months

Pricing:

- $12K-$25K/month

Expected outcome:

- leadership retains a trusted view of priorities, risks, and required decisions while internal teams execute.
