# TKO Solutions Positioning, Offer, Website, and Conversion Audit

Date: July 20, 2026  
Scope: Live-site commercial audit, competitive review, recommended positioning, offer redesign, page architecture, replacement copy, conversion plan, and Codex implementation package.  
Method: Reviewed the live desktop and 390 × 844 mobile experience across the requested routes, all six selected-work pages, navigation, footer, CTAs, contact form, sitemap, redirects, and the corresponding repository structure. No site code or production content was changed.

## 1. Blunt executive verdict

TKO has a credible operator, a serious point of view, and an unusually honest evidence standard. It does not yet have a focused commercial proposition.

The live site asks a buyer to understand a private vocabulary—“operational truth,” “human API,” “decision layer,” “operational knowledge system,” and “governed AI-assisted workflow”—before it gives that buyer a recognizable budget problem. It also presents at least four entry offers depending on the page: a three-week Operational Truth & AI Workflow Diagnostic, a one-week Prior Authorization Operational Assessment, an Operational Recovery Assessment, and an AI Delivery Assessment. The names, durations, outcomes, and buyer contexts drift. A qualified visitor can admire the thinking and still not know what to buy.

“Complex service businesses” is not a sufficiently focused market. It describes an operating characteristic, not a buying community. It leaves Todd competing simultaneously with healthcare specialists, transformation firms, AI consultancies, workflow designers, software developers, and fractional operators. TKO has not earned enough public, quantified cross-industry proof to make that breadth an advantage.

The strongest commercial wedge is **prior authorization performance for specialty medical groups, MSOs, and provider-side healthcare operators**. It is expensive, recognizable, measurable, urgent, and already connected to Todd’s healthcare operating record. The immediate exposure is not “lack of operational truth.” It is avoidable denials, slow decisions, staff time lost to rework, inconsistent payer handling, and dependence on a few experienced people.

TKO should own one public position now:

> TKO helps specialty medical groups and healthcare operators reduce prior-authorization delays, denials, and staff dependency by fixing the workflow before they buy more automation.

The public offer should have two engagements, not three:

1. **Prior Authorization Performance Diagnostic** — 15 business days, $25,000 fixed fee.
2. **90-Day Prior Authorization Improvement Sprint** — $65,000–$95,000, separately scoped after the Diagnostic.

The current one-week healthcare assessment should be merged into the Diagnostic, not marketed as a third offer. The generic Diagnostic should be renamed and narrowed. AI should become an optional implementation mechanism, not the category or the lead promise. RachelOS should remain available as method-and-governance evidence, but it should leave the homepage’s main argument and the primary navigation.

The site is technically competent on mobile: the menu works, controls are appropriately sized, and the audited pages showed no horizontal overflow. The problem is density. At 390 px wide, the homepage is roughly 14,000 px tall. The visitor meets multiple markets, six problem categories, three proof types, a proprietary method, RachelOS, a healthcare transfer argument, a founder thesis, and two offers before reaching the close. The redesign should reduce the public commercial journey to **Home → Diagnostic → Fit Call**, with case evidence supporting that path.

## 2. Ten most important problems, ranked by commercial impact

### 1. The market is defined by complexity, not by a buyer

“Complex service businesses” includes nearly every knowledge-intensive organization. It does not tell a referral partner whom to introduce, or let a buyer conclude “this firm is for companies like mine.” Healthcare, financial services, investment operations, professional services, and real estate appear in the same story without a dominant market.

**Commercial consequence:** weak referrals, low message recall, diffuse search demand, and long qualification conversations.

### 2. The site does not have one entry offer

The flagship is three weeks on `/services/diagnostic`. The prior-authorization assessment is one week. `/problems` refers to a “recovery assessment.” `/proof/transfer` says “the Assessment” is a one-week review. The RachelOS delivery case promotes an “AI Delivery Assessment.” The repository still contains retired or redirected assessment and offer routes.

**Commercial consequence:** uncertainty about scope, price class, and what Todd actually wants to sell.

### 3. The primary problem is intellectually framed rather than budget framed

Hidden handoffs, undocumented judgment, and missing operating layers are real causes. Executives more readily fund reduced denials, recovered capacity, shorter turnaround, lower rework, lower delivery risk, or a decision on whether an automation investment should proceed.

**Commercial consequence:** the buyer has to translate TKO’s method into their own business case.

### 4. AI is too prominent relative to the problem

“AI-assisted operations modernization” and “AI Workflow Diagnostic” place TKO in an overcrowded, skeptical category. The strongest parts of the offer—workflow evidence, exception analysis, decision rights, human controls, and measurable improvement—do not require AI to be the lead.

**Commercial consequence:** TKO sounds like an AI consultancy while repeatedly insisting it is not one.

### 5. Proof demonstrates method more than measurable client value

The site is admirably precise about claim boundaries, but most case-study outcomes are qualitative. RachelOS has inspectable implementation evidence, yet it is a single-operator real-estate system. Healthcare pages show relevant experience but publish no baseline, improvement, duration, scope size, or client voice.

**Commercial consequence:** procurement can verify seriousness but cannot readily estimate commercial return or delivery risk.

### 6. Healthcare and non-healthcare evidence compete instead of supporting a hierarchy

Healthcare is called a proving ground “not the boundary,” while financial services, CRE, relationship operations, investment operations, and enterprise technology are offered as portability evidence. The site spends substantial space explaining why the evidence transfers. That explanation itself signals that the proof-market fit is not obvious.

**Commercial consequence:** healthcare buyers see less specialization; non-healthcare buyers still do not see a tailored offer.

### 7. RachelOS is overused in the commercial narrative

RachelOS proves that Todd can build, govern, document, and audit a working system. It does not prove enterprise adoption, healthcare compliance, or operational ROI. Repeated “you are not buying RachelOS” explanations invite the software-company question instead of preventing it.

**Commercial consequence:** attention moves from the buyer’s problem to Todd’s reference implementation.

### 8. Implementation is a list of design abstractions, not a purchasable change

“Decision layer design,” “operational memory model,” “action queues,” and “intelligence capture workflow” do not tell an operational leader which team behavior, queue, rule, escalation, measure, or system configuration will be different by day 90.

**Commercial consequence:** the follow-on feels like bespoke consulting with uncertain effort and cost.

### 9. The CTA creates homework

“Find Your Highest-Leverage Workflow” is distinctive but vague. It sounds like a workshop or self-discovery exercise, not a clear next commercial step. The site does not state whether the next action is a sales call, a working session, a paid assessment, or a qualification review.

**Commercial consequence:** lower intent and weaker expectation setting.

### 10. Page sprawl dilutes the buying journey

`/problems`, `/proof`, `/proof/transfer`, `/frameworks`, `/healthcare`, `/selected-work`, two RachelOS cases, six case pages, and founder atlases all compete to establish the same thesis. The footer exposes eleven destinations. The homepage alone repeats the method, proof taxonomy, healthcare transfer argument, founder positioning, and engagement path.

**Commercial consequence:** qualified visitors research the firm rather than move toward a decision.

### Four-perspective read

**Executive buyer:** sees thoughtful risk language but not one exposure large enough to fund now. “Reduce PA denials and staff hours” is more actionable than “expose operational truth.”

**Operational leader:** can recognize exceptions, workarounds, and key-person risk, but cannot picture the day-90 operating change from the current implementation language.

**Procurement/risk stakeholder:** appreciates fixed duration, senior delivery, de-identified inputs, and stated exclusions. Still needs fees, staffing model, client obligations, acceptance criteria, data-handling assumptions, and a clearer distinction between advisory artifacts and production implementation.

**Skeptical referral:** cannot reliably choose between “AI workflow advisor,” “transformation recovery specialist,” “healthcare operations consultant,” and “operating-system builder.”

## 5. Recommended position and rationale

Choose **Option A: Prior authorization performance improvement**.

It is the smallest position that solves the commercial problem while preserving TKO’s real strengths. Prior authorization provides a named workflow, observable operational exposure, identifiable buyers, clear trigger events, measurable outcomes, and a credible path from diagnosis to implementation. It also lets TKO use AI appropriately: as one possible mechanism after workflow quality, evidence, exception paths, decision rights, and controls are understood.

Option B should become an expansion path after TKO has two to three strong healthcare client results or when introduced through a trusted enterprise referral. It should not share equal homepage weight now. Option C should be declined as public positioning. It is a useful internal method description, not a market category.

### Commercial decisions

| Decision | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Primary ideal customer | Multi-site specialty medical groups and MSOs with meaningful prior-authorization volume, an accountable operations/revenue-cycle owner, and access to basic submission, denial, turnaround, staffing, and payer-mix data |
| Secondary customer | Health-system or payer teams with a clearly bounded prior-authorization or utilization-management workflow; accept selectively where sponsor authority and data access are strong |
| Highest-value triggering event | Leadership is about to fund automation, change vendors, centralize PA work, respond to rising denials/backlogs, or address staff capacity—and lacks a trusted baseline or root-cause view |
| Expensive problem | PA work loses staff capacity and revenue through incomplete intake, inconsistent documentation, payer-specific variation, rework, avoidable denials, slow exceptions, and knowledge concentrated in a few people |
| Concrete result | A measured current-state baseline, prioritized causes, redesigned workflow and decision rights, quantified opportunity ranges, and a 90-day plan leadership can fund and operate |
| Flagship offer | Prior Authorization Performance Diagnostic |
| Logical follow-on | 90-Day Prior Authorization Improvement Sprint |
| Role of current healthcare assessment | Merge it into the flagship. The first week becomes evidence intake and baseline analysis; it is not a separate public product. |
| What TKO should not offer | Generic AI strategy/readiness, general workflow consulting across industries, software development as a standalone service, staff augmentation, outsourced PA operations, vendor selection alone, generic training, autonomous-agent programs, or open-ended transformation retainers |
| Referral sentence | “Todd helps specialty medical groups and healthcare operators reduce prior-authorization delays, denials, and staff dependency by fixing the workflow before they buy more automation.” |

### Positioning boundaries

- Do not imply that Todd’s employers or their clients endorse TKO.
- Do not claim quantified healthcare outcomes until the source, baseline, period, and attribution are documented.
- Do not promise denial reduction or savings before the Diagnostic establishes the baseline and addressable causes.
- Do not lead with Gold Card readiness. It is a policy/operating condition, not the dominant pain felt by most buyers.
- Do not lead with AI governance. Make controlled automation and human review part of the solution design when relevant.

### Commercial safeguards

- Diagnostic starts only with a named executive sponsor, one bounded workflow, sufficient evidence, and a decision leadership intends to make.
- TKO may recommend no implementation. That independence should be explicit.
- If specialty coding, clinical criteria, compliance interpretation, or system configuration exceeds Todd’s scope, name the required client SME or partner in the proposal.
- Use a short statement of work with acceptance based on delivery and sponsor review of defined artifacts—not promised operational savings.

## 7. Messaging system

### Category statement

Senior-led prior authorization operations advisory.

### One-sentence positioning statement

TKO helps specialty medical groups and healthcare operators reduce prior-authorization delays, denials, and staff dependency by fixing the workflow before they buy more automation.

### Primary value proposition

In 15 business days, leadership gets a measured view of where prior authorization is losing time and capacity, what is driving avoidable rework and denials, and the 90-day improvement plan worth funding.

### Three value pillars

1. **Measure the real workload.** Establish volume, effort, turnaround, rework, denial, exception, payer, and specialty patterns from evidence—not anecdotes.
2. **Fix the operating causes.** Make intake standards, decision rights, handoffs, exceptions, escalation, and ownership explicit.
3. **Automate only what is ready.** Define where rules, workflow tooling, or AI can safely help—and where human or clinical judgment must remain.

### Primary buyer pain

“We know prior authorization is consuming too much staff time and delaying care or revenue, but we cannot agree on which part of the workflow is causing it or what to fix first.”

### Triggering events

- Denials, backlog, or turnaround deteriorate.
- Experienced staff are overloaded or leaving.
- Leadership plans to centralize or standardize PA operations.
- A vendor renewal, EHR change, or automation investment is approaching.
- Different specialties or locations perform materially differently.
- A payer-policy change or Gold Card initiative exposes workflow inconsistency.
- A prior automation pilot has not produced a defensible result.

### Differentiators

- Principal-led from intake through readout and, if selected, implementation.
- Workflow and decision-rights diagnosis before technology recommendation.
- Healthcare operating, interoperability, product, and transformation experience in one accountable advisor.
- Vendor-neutral and fixed-scope; no platform quota or staffing bench to feed.
- Claim discipline: baseline and opportunity ranges are documented before outcomes are promised.
- Implementation remains bounded to the selected workflow and measurable pilot.

### Proof points

- 20+ years in regulated operations, product, platform, and transformation roles.
- Healthcare experience spanning prior authorization, utilization management, payer/provider workflows, CMS interoperability, and enterprise modernization.
- Career record includes Apollo Global Management, Sapient work involving Goldman Sachs Asset Management and JPMorgan Asset Management, FolioDynamix, ELLKAY, and Cognizant. Employment history is not client endorsement.
- RachelOS demonstrates Todd’s ability to implement source authority, human approval, explainable next actions, and operating-health controls in a live system. It is method evidence, not healthcare or enterprise-scale proof.

### Objection responses

**“We already know our process.”**  
Most teams know the documented process. The Diagnostic tests where actual cases deviate by payer, specialty, evidence quality, exception, and escalation—and measures which deviations matter.

**“Our vendor already provides workflow analytics.”**  
Vendor analytics describe activity inside the platform. TKO examines the work across intake, documentation, people, payer rules, queues, systems, and decisions, including work the platform does not capture.

**“We need automation, not another assessment.”**  
The Diagnostic is designed to make the automation decision: what is stable enough to automate, what must be redesigned first, what requires human judgment, and what value range justifies the investment.

**“Can you guarantee denial reduction?”**  
No responsible advisor should before establishing which denials are avoidable and operationally addressable. TKO guarantees the defined analysis and decision package, then measures a pilot against the agreed baseline.

**“Why Todd rather than a large firm?”**  
You work directly with the principal who conducts the interviews, interprets the evidence, designs the recommendation, and stays accountable through the readout. TKO is appropriate for one bounded, high-consequence workflow—not an enterprise staffing program.

### CTA system

- Primary CTA: **Request a Diagnostic Fit Call**
- Secondary CTA: **See Scope & Pricing**
- Proof-page CTA: **See the Prior Authorization Diagnostic**
- Contact-submit CTA: **Request the Fit Call**
- Implementation CTA: **Discuss the 90-Day Sprint**

### Language rules

| Retire from lead messaging | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Operational truth | What actually happens in the workflow; measured current state |
| Human API | Key-person dependency; knowledge concentrated in a few people |
| Governed AI-assisted workflow | Controlled automation or AI with defined human review |
| Operational knowledge system | Shared rules, documented knowledge, and workflow controls |
| Decision layer | Decision rights, escalation, and next action |
| Highest-leverage workflow | The PA workflow under pressure; request a fit call |
| AI-assisted operations modernization | Prior authorization operations advisory |

“Operational truth” may remain once as a method label in an About or methodology section. “Human API” can remain in thought leadership if defined, but not in offer names, CTAs, or deliverables. “RachelOS” remains a proper name on its proof page only.

1. Homepage identifies PA buyer, cost, result, proof, starting offer, and CTA.

### Category conventions

- The market expects a visible specialization, not “complex work.”
- Senior-led must name what the principal personally does.
- Diagnostics need duration, inputs, outputs, decision, and price class.
- Implementation needs a concrete end state, client dependencies, measures, and handoff.
- Healthcare credibility is expressed through domain depth, named roles, client voice, and measured operating/financial outcomes.
- “Vendor-neutral,” “no platform to sell,” and “fixed scope” are credible only when the service path does not look like a disguised build funnel.

### White space TKO can own

The large healthcare firms are broad, team-based, and enterprise-scale. The boutique AI firms are often clear and commercially bounded but horizontally positioned. TKO can occupy the gap: **a principal-led, fixed-fee prior authorization workflow diagnosis that is operational, healthcare-literate, vendor-neutral, and capable of staying through one 90-day improvement.**
