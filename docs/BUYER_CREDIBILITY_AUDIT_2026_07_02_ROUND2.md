# TKO Website Buyer Credibility Audit — Round 2

**Date:** 2026-07-02
**Method:** Follow-up review of the same buyer-facing site content, reframed per-page (trust increases / decreases / unfinished / doubt / missing proof / unanswered objections), plus direct hire/refer/book verdicts and three ranked 20-hour fix lists (credibility, conversion, recruiter/hiring-manager). Evaluated as a skeptical buyer with no prior relationship, no referral, no existing trust — website only.

Corrects one Round 1 assumption: `/industries` is a clean redirect to `/selected-work`, not a live orphaned page. The `industries` content array and its `IndustryPattern` component exist in the codebase but are never rendered on any route — a dead component, not a broken nav item.

---

## Homepage

**Increases trust:** The problem list (Human API dependency, transformation recovery, administrative burden) is specific and recognizable — reads like someone who has actually lived inside these workflows. Fixed pricing visible via the services flow is unusual and welcome.

**Decreases trust:** The hero sidebar is titled "Proof Ladder" and literally narrates its own persuasion sequence: *"The page moves from healthcare credibility to built-system proof to second-domain portability, then asks leaders to start with a fixed-scope recovery assessment."* That's a funnel-design memo, not a pitch. Section eyebrows ("Healthcare Credibility," "Proof Ladder / 1," "Proof Ladder / 2") repeat the tell. "Primary conversion path" is a literal badge on the page.

**Feels unfinished:** The RachelOS proof card and CRE proof card sit at equal visual weight to real enterprise claims, with no indication of what either actually is.

**Creates doubt:** Watching a stranger show his own strategy notes reframes everything below as a sales mechanic rather than evidence.

**Proof missing:** No name, no face, no client, no number, on the page a buyer sees first.

**Unanswered objections:** Who am I dealing with? Has anyone paid for this?

---

## About

**Increases trust:** Nothing significant. The "method" (asking better operating questions) is fine but generic.

**Decreases trust:** Zero identity. No last name, no photo, no LinkedIn, no employer names, no dates, no team sizes, no program budgets. "Phases" are written as abstract nouns rather than a résumé. The headline — *"The category came after the operating work"* — defends a positioning decision no buyer asked about.

**Feels unfinished:** The least finished page on the site, and the one page whose entire job is establishing who's being trusted.

**Creates doubt:** For a Director/VP hire, a fractional advisor, or a networking contact, this page is the whole ballgame and currently answers none of it. Raises the question of whether "Todd" is even findable, or a persona over something the anonymity protects.

**Proof missing:** Full name. Headshot. LinkedIn. Named employer classes. Scale numbers.

**Unanswered objections:** Why should I trust an anonymous bio? Is this person even searchable?

## Services (index) + the four service pages (Recovery Assessment, Diagnostic, Build, Fractional)

**Increases trust:** The strongest part of the site. Fixed price, fixed duration, explicit deliverables, explicit exclusions, honest FAQs ("Can this be bought cold? No.").

**Decreases trust:** The page hero literally displays the internal funnel-stage label as the eyebrow: Build says **"Downstream conversion offer,"** Fractional says **"Post-diagnostic or post-build retainer."** Notes-to-self about deal flow, shown to the buyer as the page's identity. "Rung 1 / Rung 2" ladder framing treats the buyer as a funnel stage.

**Feels unfinished:** None of the "deliverables" (workflow map, executive briefing, decision layer design) are ever shown.

**Creates doubt:** Prices up to $150K and $25K/month sit next to zero named clients.

**Proof missing:** One redacted sample deliverable. One dollar or hour figure from a past engagement.

**Unanswered objections:** What do I physically receive? Why is a $45–150K build priced as if proven, with nothing to verify?

---

## Prior Authorization Assessment (healthcare specialization page)

**Increases trust:** The best page on the site. Specific inputs (denial reasons, payer mix, staffing model), specific outputs, buyer-voiced questions, tight FAQ, two linked insight articles backing the claim with real reasoning.

**Decreases trust:** Nothing structural — this page mostly avoids the sitewide funnel-language problem.

**Feels unfinished:** Still no proof this exact assessment has been run on a real prior-auth workflow before.

**Proof missing:** A single anonymized number would make this page convert on its own.

**Unanswered objections:** Has this specific assessment ever actually been delivered, or is it a well-designed offer that hasn't shipped?

---

## Selected Work (index + case study detail pages)

**Increases trust:** The four-part structure (Situation / Operating Truth / What Changed / Result) shows real diagnostic thinking.

**Decreases trust:** Every case study ends with some version of "no client names or unverifiable metrics are published." Once is discretion; five times is a pattern reading as "nothing to verify." RachelOS — the flagship, "Featured proof" — turns out to be "a relationship-driven business" with "100+ relationships," never disclosed as the founder's own tool. Once a buyer figures that out, it recolors every other case study with suspicion.

**Feels unfinished:** The index page promises "Common Pattern" sections with essentially no content underneath. The CRE Intelligence case study openly states it exists to "demonstrate method portability" rather than solve a client's problem.

**Proof missing:** Screenshots of RachelOS (it's software — show it). One scale number per case study.

**Unanswered objections:** Is RachelOS a client engagement or a personal project? Why should personal-project proof carry the same weight as unverifiable claimed enterprise work?

---

## Insights

**Increases trust:** Genuinely good writing — "Human APIs Become Organizational Bottlenecks" and "Prior Authorization Is a Decision-Rights Problem" are sharp and demonstrate real domain fluency. The most authentic voice on the site.

**Decreases trust:** All four posts dated the same day. No byline, no author photo — on a site with no name anywhere, an unattributed article proves nothing about the author's expertise.

**Feels unfinished:** A four-post, single-day archive reads as a content sprint timed to a review, not an accumulated body of work.

**Proof missing:** Author attribution tied back to a real About-page identity.

**Unanswered objections:** Did a person with real experience write this, or is this AI-assisted content wearing expertise language?

---

## Contact

**Increases trust:** Clear, well-scoped five-question intake; sets expectations about specificity.

**Decreases trust:** No calendar link, no email, no phone. The only contact path is five paragraph-length answers to a stranger who hasn't given a last name. Copy like *"The Assessment starts only when the workflow problem is concrete"* and *"More detail is required"* reads as gatekeeping from a brand that hasn't earned the right to gatekeep yet.

**Feels unfinished:** No confirmation of what happens next.

**Proof missing:** A visible human contact channel that doesn't require a five-essay toll.

**Unanswered objections:** If I submit this, does anything happen? Is there a real person checking this inbox?

# Direct Answers

**1. Would I hire Todd (Director/VP)?**
No. Cannot run the basic diligence loop every hiring manager runs before a first call — name, LinkedIn, verified employer history. The site withholds the one input that decision requires.

**2. Would I hire TKO (consulting engagement)?**
No, not cold. The offer design (fixed scope, fixed price, clear deliverables) is good enough to say yes if a colleague vouched first — but the site alone doesn't clear the bar. Expert-sounding language, zero verifiable evidence behind it.

**3. Would I refer Todd?**
No. Referring spends personal credibility. Can't stake reputation on a person who can't be identified or verified, regardless of how good the problem framing reads.

**4. Would I book a call?**
No — and mechanically can't anyway; there's no calendar link. Writing five essays to a persona before getting a human response is too much friction for zero identity confirmation in return.

## Bottom Line

The expertise is real — the problem-diagnosis writing (Human API, prior-auth decision rights) is the best material on the site and wouldn't be easy to fake. But the site currently protects the founder's identity as carefully as it should be protecting client confidentiality, and does the opposite of what it needs to: it exposes internal funnel strategy to the buyer while hiding the one thing — a name — that would make everything else believable. Total fix cost for the top-tier items across all three lists is well under a 20-hour budget, and roughly half of it (full identity, honest RachelOS framing, removing funnel language, one deliverable sample) is copy-only work, not engineering. That's the whole gap between Definitely No and Probably Yes.
