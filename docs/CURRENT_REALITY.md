## Purpose

This document defines the starting point for Human API Risk Assessment as a documentation-first TKO Solutions offering. It separates what is known today from what is hypothesized, what requires market validation, and what could eventually become software.

## Core Observation

Across enterprise healthcare transformation, program and portfolio management, RachelOS, and CRE intelligence exploration, the same operating pattern appears:

Organizations rely on a small number of individuals who act as the unofficial interface between people, systems, processes, decisions, exceptions, and memory.

These individuals are Human APIs.

They are not merely subject matter experts. They perform operational functions that should be visible, governed, transferable, and measurable:

- They remember what the system does not.
- They route work when the workflow is unclear.
- They translate ambiguous decisions into action.
- They know which exception path actually works.
- They keep stalled work moving through personal follow-up.
- They explain what leadership reports cannot show.
- They bridge the gap between formal process and operational reality.

## Why This Matters

Human APIs create resilience in the short term and fragility in the long term.

They are often the reason work gets done despite broken systems. They are also the reason organizations cannot scale, automate, modernize, or adopt AI safely. The knowledge and routing logic required to run the business lives in people rather than in governed operating systems.

When a Human API is unavailable, overloaded, promoted, disengaged, or leaves the organization:

- Work stalls.
- Decisions slow down.
- Escalations become inconsistent.
- Program risk rises without early warning.
- AI initiatives lack the operational context needed to be useful.
- Transformation programs drift back into meetings, spreadsheets, and personal follow-up.

## Enterprise Value Implication

Human API dependency is also an enterprise value and exit readiness risk.

Many organizations are worth less than leadership believes when critical operational knowledge, customer relationships, workflow routing, decision logic, and execution capability are trapped inside a small number of people. The same conditions that make work hard to scale, modernize, or automate also make a business harder for buyers, investors, private equity firms, search funds, and acquirers to trust.

This does not create a separate near-term offer. It is the valuation consequence of the existing Human API thesis:

- Founder or key-employee dependency reduces transferability.
- Operational opacity increases buyer diligence risk.
- Tribal knowledge makes scale and integration harder.
- Undocumented workflows make performance harder to verify.
- Missing organizational memory weakens continuity after a sale, leadership change, or acquisition.

Healthcare Operational Recovery remains the primary wedge. Enterprise Value & Exit Readiness should be treated as a future extension for business owners, founders, healthcare practice owners, multi-location healthcare groups, private equity, search funds, business brokers, M&A advisors, and fractional operators.

## What Does Not Exist Yet

The Human API Risk Assessment is not yet a software product and should not be presented as one.

Missing or not yet validated:

- A completed named client Human API assessment.
- A validated scoring model with benchmark data.
- A repeatable assessment workbook tested across multiple organizations.
- A packaged sales deck or executive readout template.
- A software application, database, automated survey engine, or analytics platform.
- Published thought leadership specifically naming and owning the Human API category.

## Tier-1 Healthcare Use Case: Prior Authorization Operational Assessment

Prior Authorization Operational Assessment is a Tier-1 TKO opportunity because it sits directly at the intersection of Human APIs, Administrative Burden, Healthcare Transformation, AI Adoption, and Operational Assessments.

The wedge is operational quality improvement, not Gold Card software, prior authorization automation software, payer technology, FHIR infrastructure, or claims adjudication.

Core market observation:

- Practice administrators do not wake up asking for Gold Card programs, FHIR integrations, or prior authorization platforms.
- They ask why they are getting denied, why authorization takes so long, why staff spend hours on manual work, why some providers get approved and others do not, why certain payers create friction, and why work depends on specific people.

Gold Card readiness is an output of operational quality. It is not the product.

## Strategic Constraint

This is not knowledge management.

Knowledge management asks, "Where should we store what people know?"

Human API Risk Assessment asks, "Where has the organization become operationally dependent on specific people, and what business risk does that create?"

The deliverable is a risk and remediation model, not a wiki.

## Near-Term Definition Of Success

The offering becomes useful if it can help an executive answer these questions:

- Which people are carrying operational dependency risk?
- Which workflows would slow down or fail if those people were unavailable?
- Which decisions, escalations, and exception paths are undocumented or manually routed?
- Which modernization or AI efforts are blocked by hidden human dependency?
- What should be captured, redesigned, governed, or automated first?

## Evidence Boundaries

Do not invent metrics for this offering.

Acceptable evidence today:

- Qualitative patterns from documented TKO experience.
- Anonymized case study outlines.
- RachelOS as proof of operating pattern.
- Healthcare background as domain credibility.
- Hypothesis language for unvalidated claims.

Not acceptable:

- Benchmark percentages without source data.
- ROI claims without an assessed client baseline.
- Claims that a software product exists.
- Claims that the scoring model is validated across a large dataset.
