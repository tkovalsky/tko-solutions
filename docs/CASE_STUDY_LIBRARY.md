# Human API Risk Assessment Case Study Library

## Evidence Rules

These are case study outlines, not public case studies.

Do not invent metrics. Do not imply client authorization. Do not name organizations unless permission exists. Use these as structured evidence patterns for sales, content, and future validation.

## 1. Enterprise Healthcare Transformation

### Problem

Large healthcare transformation programs span multiple teams, systems, workflows, and regulatory constraints. Formal status reporting may show progress while real delivery risk accumulates in dependencies, decision delays, and exception paths.

### Human API Pattern

A small number of transformation leaders or operators become the only people who understand:

- Which dependencies actually matter.
- Which teams are blocked.
- Which system is trusted for which information.
- Which executive decision is needed.
- Which risk is real versus noise.

### Risk

- Cross-team dependencies become invisible.
- Executive status becomes disconnected from operational truth.
- Decisions are delayed because context must be reconstructed manually.
- Program continuity depends on one or two people.

### Intervention

- Map formal status versus operational reality.
- Identify dependency-layer Human APIs.
- Document decision rights and escalation paths.
- Create an operational truth map for sponsor decisions.
- Prioritize remediation around critical dependencies and decision latency.

### Outcome

Outcome should be documented only with verified engagement evidence. Until then, describe the expected result as improved visibility, clearer escalation, and a prioritized recovery plan.

### Lessons Learned

Transformation risk often lives between teams, not inside individual workstreams. A dashboard can be green while the operating model is fragile.

## 2. Program Governance

### Problem

Program and portfolio governance often relies on meetings, manual status updates, and a few experienced coordinators who translate scattered information into executive narratives.

### Human API Pattern

The program lead becomes the interface between workstreams, reports, risks, decisions, and sponsors.

They know:

- Which status updates are credible.
- Which risks need escalation.
- Which teams are blocked.
- Which unresolved decision is slowing execution.
- Which informal commitment matters.

### Risk

- Governance depends on personal interpretation.
- Decisions lack durable records.
- Escalations happen late.
- Sponsor confidence depends on one person's ability to synthesize complexity.

### Intervention

- Inventory recurring governance meetings and decision artifacts.
- Map decisions, dependencies, and escalation paths.
- Separate status reporting from operational risk management.
- Create a decision log and dependency risk model.

### Outcome

Outcome should be described only after validation. Expected result is a clearer governance layer that reduces reliance on one coordinator as the operating system.

### Lessons Learned

Governance is not the meeting cadence. Governance is the durable model for decisions, ownership, escalation, evidence, and follow-through.

## 3. RachelOS

### Problem

A relationship-driven business had lead capture and digital presence, but follow-up depended on memory, manual reconstruction, scattered context, and the operator's ability to know who needed attention.

### Human API Pattern

The operator functioned as the Human API for relationship memory and next action:

- Remembering prior interactions.
- Knowing which relationship mattered.
- Deciding what to do next.
- Identifying missing context.
- Translating scattered signals into follow-up.

### Risk

- Leads could stall without visibility.
- Relationship context could disappear across channels.
- Follow-up quality depended on manual memory.
- AI drafting would be risky without human approval and structured facts.

### Intervention

RachelOS established a pattern for:

- Relationship memory.
- Fact extraction.
- Source authority.
- Canonical next action.
- Intelligence gap detection.
- Human approval before AI-assisted outreach.
- Operational alerting.

### Outcome

Confirmed outcome: a working operating pattern exists for turning scattered signals into structured facts, next actions, and human-approved workflow support.

Do not claim revenue, conversion, appointment, or transaction impact without verified data.

### Lessons Learned

The reusable pattern is not real estate-specific. The durable lesson is that relationship-intensive work needs memory, facts, state, canonical action, and human approval.

## 4. CRE Intelligence

### Problem

Commercial real estate intelligence work depends on market knowledge, relationship context, property signals, timing, and interpretation of fragmented data. The concept is relevant to Human API risk because market intelligence often lives with experienced individuals.

### Human API Pattern

Experienced operators may become the interface between:

- Market signals.
- Relationship intelligence.
- Deal context.
- Tenant or owner history.
- Timing judgment.
- Opportunity prioritization.

### Risk

- Market knowledge remains tacit.
- Opportunity decisions depend on individual memory.
- Intelligence products cannot scale without a repeatable model.
- A software or report product would be premature without validated workflows and data sources.

### Intervention

For now, intervention should remain diagnostic:

- Identify what intelligence is actually used.
- Map source data, human interpretation, and decision outputs.
- Determine whether repeatable knowledge assets exist.
- Validate whether the market wants an assessment, report, advisory, or product.

### Outcome

No outcome should be claimed until there is a concrete CRE engagement, dataset, client, or deliverable.

### Lessons Learned

CRE is a useful adjacent pattern but should not be positioned as primary proof until evidence exists. Treat it as a future validation path, not a current case study.
